'

My dear [Bea (Beatrice Henrietta Heathcote)](../people/655.html), It must have been the Die and not the seal itself that was hidden in the wall. [Lottie Yonge (Charlotte Fortescue Yonge)](../people/1588.html) has discovered at the Croft Vertue's Cromwell seals and medals with Richard's seal - he is on horseback on one side and in parliament on the other. Also that Timbs in his [*'Abbeys and Castles' (Abbeys, Castles and Ancient halls of England and Wales)*](../bibliography/general/739.html)says that 'Wllm Heathcote Esq found the die and sold it but [Sir]{.underline} William Heathcote bought the die.[^1] This is on the authority of Vertue in 1760, but as he thought it was the die of the seal of the Long Parliament.

I do not much hold to his authority, and if the seal or die was bought back to Hursley, what could have become of it?

I think the actual seal is a flat thing, like a coin (only concave) and the die big and solid more like a weight.

[Anna (Anna Rachel Bramston)](../people/195.html) has sent me the rubbing and it is gone to [Edmund Morshead (Edmund Doidge Anderson Morshead)](../people/42.html). The Frances White who erected the monument to Sir Charles Wyndham must have been the wife of the man with the Crease

I have written to [Mr Chamberlayne (Tankerville Chamberlayne)](../people/2114.html) to ask after Sir Isaac Newton's sun dial. I think - and so does [Alethea (Louisa Alethea Bowles)](../people/182.html) that there used to be one at Cranbury on the slope above King's Lane

Boyatt Lane and High bridge used to belong to Brambridge so I can get in the avenues being pollarded for gun stocks, and Mrs Fitzherbert whom my [mother (Frances Mary Yonge)](../people/1606.html)saw there and remembered her eyebrows

Yours affectionately C M Yonge

---

[^1]: The research material was being gathered for *<i>John Keble's Parishes</i>*, and the story of the discovery of the Great Seal of the Commonwealth appears on pp.50-1.  On page 74 there is a description of the monument to Thomas White 'with a blade waved like a Malay crease'.  Maria Fitzherbert, the supposed wife of George IV, belonged to the  Smythe family who owned Brambridge;  they are mentioned on pp. 85-7.
